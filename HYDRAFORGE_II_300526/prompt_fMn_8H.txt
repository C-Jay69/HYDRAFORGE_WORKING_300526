M&A CONTRACT RISK ASSESSMENT REPORT
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This contract for the acquisition of assets presents significant risks for the Buyer. While some aspects, like the earnout calculation, are well-defined, the extensive MAE carve-outs, broad assumption of liabilities, limitations on representations and warranties, and lack of a working capital adjustment mechanism create a highly unfavorable risk profile for the Buyer. Major revisions are necessary before proceeding.

FINAL SCORE & VERDICT
Risk Score: 55/100 Risk Level: High Recommendation: Proceed with Major Revisions

CRITICAL FINDINGS (Must Address)
The "as is, where is" transfer of assets combined with the broad assumption of liabilities, specifically the clause "any liability arising from the ownership or operation of the Acquired Assets that is not expressly listed as an Excluded Liability shall be deemed an Assumed Liability," creates a severe blind assumption of risk for the Buyer.
Seller's indemnification obligations are limited to "actual knowledge" as of Closing, making it extremely difficult for the Buyer to recover for breaches of representations and warranties.
The combined impact of the "as is, where is" clause, the "exclusive remedy" for fraud, and the Buyer's "Independent Investigation" clause effectively eliminates Buyer's recourse.
MODERATE FINDINGS (Should Negotiate)
MAE definition includes several broad carve-outs (general economic conditions, industry-wide changes, announcement of transaction, pandemic, buyer actions) that significantly limit Buyer's ability to invoke it.
Lack of a specific working capital target or mechanism in the purchase price adjustment.
Definition of 'Acquired Assets' is broad but relies heavily on schedules, which are not provided.
Rep 3.3 (Title to Assets) is qualified by 'material to the Business'.
Rep 3.6 (Contracts) uses 'material default' and 'to the Knowledge of Seller'.
Rep 3.8 (Environmental Compliance) uses 'To the Knowledge of Seller' and 'to the Knowledge of Seller'.
Rep 3.11 (Customers and Suppliers) relies on 'Seller has not received written notice'.
BLIND SPOTS IDENTIFIED
Insurance Coverage: Neither review mentioned the need to verify the Seller's insurance coverage and ensure adequate tail coverage for pre-closing events.
Permits and Licenses: Neither review addressed the transferability of necessary permits and licenses to operate the acquired assets.
Litigation Search: Neither review mentioned the need to conduct a thorough litigation search to identify any undisclosed or potential liabilities.
DETAILED ANALYSIS BY CHECKLIST POINT
1. Definitions & Recitals
The MAE carve-outs are extensive and shift significant risk to the Buyer. The definition of "Acquired Assets" relies heavily on schedules that are not provided. The definition of "Assumed Liabilities" is dangerously broad, particularly the clause 'any liability arising from the ownership or operation of the Acquired Assets that is not expressly listed as an Excluded Liability shall be deemed an Assumed Liability,' which could pull in unknown liabilities. This is a critical issue.

2. Purchase Price & Consideration
The Earnout Calculation section is well-defined, including specific exclusions for Buyer synergies/new products, good faith operation covenant, and a clear dispute resolution mechanism. However, the contract lacks any explicit working capital adjustment mechanism or target, which is a significant omission.

3. Representations & Warranties
Seller's reps contain several problematic materiality and knowledge qualifiers. The 'as is, where is' clause for assets, combined with the 'exclusive remedy' for fraud, significantly weakens Buyer's post-closing recourse. The Buyer's independent investigation clause further limits reliance. Seller's indemnification for reps is limited to losses arising from matters of which Seller had 'actual knowledge' as of Closing, which is a very high bar for Buyer to prove.

WHAT YOUR ANALYZER GETS WRONG OR OVERSTATES

This is where professional-grade refinement matters.

1. Your analyzer overstates the MAE problem

You repeatedly say:

MAE is practically useless.

That is overstated.

The carveouts are actually fairly market-standard for sophisticated PE/M&A deals.

The REAL issue is:

absence of a “disproportionate effects” carveback.

That’s the doctrinal defect.

Normally:
industry/economic carveouts exclude broad market events,
BUT:
if target suffers disproportionately relative to peers,
buyer regains protection.

Your analyzer noticed absence of carveback but underweighted it.

That should be the centerpiece criticism.

Not:
“MAE useless.”

Because Delaware-style MAE clauses are intentionally narrow.

2. “No source code escrow” is mostly a false positive

This is one of your weakest calls.

You flagged:

no source code escrow arrangement.

In a full acquisition of a SaaS company,
Buyer OWNS the company and its source code after closing.

Source code escrow matters primarily in:

licensing deals,
strategic partnerships,
critical vendor arrangements,
bankruptcy continuity.

In a stock/membership acquisition:
this is usually irrelevant.

This is a classic example of:

checklist contamination.

Your model likely imported a SaaS transactional checklist without contextual weighting.

You need contextual suppression logic.

3. “No TSA” is probably incorrect / weak

Your:

“Day-1 Operational Viability”

analysis overstates TSA necessity.

This is a membership interest purchase.

The entity survives intact.

Not typically in 100% equity acquisitions of standalone SaaS firms.

This is another “template risk inflation” issue.
